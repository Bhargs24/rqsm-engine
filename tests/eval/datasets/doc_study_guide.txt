STUDY GUIDE: TCP CONGESTION CONTROL

OVERVIEW

This guide covers the core mechanisms used by TCP to share network
bandwidth fairly between competing flows. By the end of this guide you
should be able to explain slow start, congestion avoidance, fast
retransmit and fast recovery in your own words, and you should be able to
recognise the typical sawtooth pattern in a TCP throughput plot.

KEY CONCEPTS

The fundamental problem TCP solves is that the sender does not know the
capacity of the path between itself and the receiver. The sender therefore
has to discover the capacity by probing. The probing is conservative on
the way up and aggressive on the way down. This is the asymmetry that
gives TCP its characteristic behaviour. Two state variables drive the
mechanism. The congestion window cwnd bounds the number of unacknowledged
bytes in flight from the sender. The slow start threshold ssthresh marks
the boundary between exponential and linear growth.

SLOW START

Slow start runs at the beginning of a connection and after a timeout
indicates that the network state has been lost. The sender begins with a
small congestion window, typically ten segments in modern stacks. Every
time an acknowledgment arrives, the congestion window grows by one
segment, which means the window doubles every round trip time. This is
exponential growth in disguise. The phase ends either when an
acknowledgment is missed or when the congestion window crosses the slow
start threshold.

CONGESTION AVOIDANCE

Congestion avoidance is the linear growth phase. Once the congestion
window has crossed the slow start threshold, every round trip time it
grows by one segment instead of doubling. This is the additive increase
side of the additive increase multiplicative decrease pattern. The
network is now at or near capacity and growing aggressively would push
all flows into loss simultaneously, which is exactly what TCP wants to
avoid.

FAST RETRANSMIT AND FAST RECOVERY

A timeout is an expensive way to detect packet loss because it stalls
the connection for at least one timeout period. Fast retransmit shortcuts
this by interpreting three duplicate acknowledgments as a strong signal
that a single segment was lost. The sender retransmits the missing
segment immediately without waiting for the timeout. Fast recovery then
takes over. Instead of resetting the congestion window all the way down
to the initial value, fast recovery sets the slow start threshold to
half the current congestion window, sets the congestion window to the
new threshold and resumes congestion avoidance directly. This avoids the
costly slow start phase when a single segment is lost.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS

A common misconception is that TCP throughput is limited by the bandwidth
of the bottleneck link. The actual ceiling is the product of the round
trip time and the maximum congestion window allowed by the receiver and
by the sender. On a high bandwidth long delay path the bandwidth-delay
product can be tens of megabytes, which is far larger than the default
window size on many systems.

Another misconception is that any packet loss indicates congestion. On
wireless links and on lossy intermediate paths, packets may be lost
because of bit errors rather than congestion. Standard TCP cannot
distinguish between these two causes and reduces the congestion window
in both cases. This is part of why specialised congestion control
algorithms exist for wireless and high speed environments.

WORKED EXAMPLES

Consider a connection with a one hundred millisecond round trip time and
a one megabyte per second bottleneck link. The bandwidth-delay product is
one hundred kilobytes. The sender therefore needs to keep at least one
hundred kilobytes in flight to fully utilise the link. With a maximum
segment size of one and a half kilobytes, this is approximately seventy
segments. If the congestion window is held below seventy segments, the
sender cannot fill the pipe regardless of the available bandwidth.

As a second example, consider a connection that experiences a single
segment loss after the congestion window has grown to forty segments.
Fast retransmit triggers on the third duplicate acknowledgment. The slow
start threshold drops to twenty segments. The congestion window also
drops to twenty segments. Congestion avoidance resumes from twenty.
Without fast recovery, the connection would have entered slow start from
ten segments and lost roughly two round trip times of throughput.

REVIEW QUESTIONS

Try to answer these in your own words before moving on. What is the
difference between the slow start phase and the congestion avoidance
phase, and what triggers the transition between them? Why does fast
recovery skip slow start after a single segment loss? Under what
circumstances does TCP misinterpret packet loss as congestion, and what
are the consequences for throughput?

SUMMARY

TCP congestion control is built around two state variables, cwnd and
ssthresh, and four mechanisms, slow start, congestion avoidance, fast
retransmit and fast recovery. The mechanisms together implement an
additive increase multiplicative decrease policy that probes the network
capacity conservatively on the way up and reduces aggressively on the
way down. The result is the familiar sawtooth pattern in steady state
throughput plots. Most modern variants tweak the constants and the
recovery rules but preserve the additive increase multiplicative decrease
shape.
